Major Scales

the C major Scale

As we already know, the natural notes form a C major scale. That is to say that they form a series of 8 notes, with tones and semitones arranged in this way: semitones between notes 3-4 and 7-8. This is really what a major scale is, and it's important that you memorise that.

You might have noticed that the bottom half of the scale looks a lot like the top half. We have two sets of 4 notes, with the the semitones between notes 3 and 4. A set of 4 notes is called a tetrachord. One very helpful way to understand the Major scale is to see it as two tetrachord like that.

Here is the c major scale again, this time on 2 octaves, with the two tetrachords and the semitones highlighted.

Now, let's try to make a major scale starting on a different note. In principle, I could pick any note to start my new scale, but I'm going to use the fact that the two tetrachords are the same and I'm going to do it so that the top tetrachord of C major becomes the bottom tetrachord of my new scale. By doing it this way, the first four notes of my new scale are already done. Our scale will start g-a-b-c.

G is note 1 of the scale so it will be a G major scale. If we just use all natural notes, we don't have a G major scale, because we don't have the second semitone in the right place.

But it's very close! All we need to do is raise F to an F♯ and there we have it.

G major scale

Now we have all the tones and semitones in the right place. So this is a G major scale.

The process we have just carried out to get the G major scale from the C major scale, we can now repeat with the G major scale, to obtain the D major scale.

Notice that what we are doing each time is making a new scale that starts on note 5 (a perfect fifth above) of the previous scale, and adding the extra sharp we need to note 7 of the new scale (or note 4 of the old one).

We can keep doing this to form all of the following scales.

A major

E major

B major

F sharp major

C sharp major

Scales with flats

In the previous section we formed a lot of different major scales, all with sharps. In this section we will form all of the major scales which have flats.

We will start again from C major, and this time we will form a new scale such that the top tetrachord of our new scale is the bottom tetrachord of C major. This is the other way round to what we did in the previous section.

C-D-E-F will be notes 5,6,7,8 of our new scale which means that our scale will be F major (note 1 and 8 are the same)

Starting on F with just natural notes we don't quite have a major scale, but it's very close! We just need to lower the B to a B♭.

Now we have all the tones and semitones in the rigth place, so we have an F major scale.

We can keep doing this to each new scale that we make: Creating a new major scale by starting on note 4 of the previous scale (or a perfect fifth below) and flattening note 4 of the new scale (or note 7 of the old scale).

If we keep repeating this we obtain all the following major scales.

B flat major

E flat major

A flat major

D flat major

G flat major

C flat major

The circle of fifths

Each time we add a sharp or flat, we are going up or down a fifth.

Notice that the key with 6 sharps is F# major and 6 flats is G flat major. G flat and F sharp are the same pitch (both between F and G) We call these enharmonic equivalents. C# and D flat, and B and C flat are also enharmonic equivalents. If we fold the table on itself so that the enharmonic equievalents meet, we get the following.

maybe this should go with enharmonic equivalents??

This phenomenon is known as the circle of fifths.

It is important that you understand this. You should be able to name any major key and its key signature. If this seems very difficult, realise that all you need to memorise is that C major has no sharps or flats, and that it all works in fifths: you go up a fifth and you have one more sharp, you go down a fifth and you have one more flat.

Aside: as a tip that might help you identify key signatures for major scales:
- no major scale without flat in the name has any flats, except f major
- for a major scale with sharps, the last sharp in the key sign is the seventh note
- for a major scale with flats, the tonic is the second-to-last flat

Minor Scales

We have leanrt about major scales. There is another kind of scale we need to learn about: minor sclaes.

Minor scales are a bit more complicated because there are 3 forms of the minor scale that we need to learn: natural, harmonic and melodic.

A minor

The minor scale with no shaprs and flats is A minor natural.

When we say raise, we mean raise by a semitone with an accidetal, so an F would become an F#, an Eb would become an E. For the melodic scale, we will see that it is not the same going up and going down (we usually use the words ascending and descending). ### harmonic minors To form a harmonic scale, we take the natural minor and we raise note 7 (going up and going down) EXAmples: a minor C minor G# minor others When you write harmonic scales like this, make sure you write the accidental on note 7 going down as well. ### melodic minors For the melodic scale, we will start with the natural minor and we will raise notes 6 and 7 GOING UP ONLY. This means that going down, the melodic is exactly the same as the natural. Examples: A mninor C minor G# minor When you write out minor melodic scales like this, make sure you write accidentals on notes 6 and 7 going down as well, just to avoid any confusion. ### why are these notes raised in the harmonic and melodic scales? ## keys - recogninsing / understanding ## building your intuition for scales play around, goign from major to minor melodic or harmonic, think about the intervals fomred between notes in the various scales. ## a note on key signatures It's important not to confuse the raised 6th and 7th notes with the key signature.
