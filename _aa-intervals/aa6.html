To invert something means to turn it upside down. In the case of intervals, it means that if we have an interval with, say, A above D, we will flip those around so that we have D above A.

We can do this by moving the bottom note up an octave, or by moving the top note down an octave.

Try playing around with this. Write down a few intervals, identify them, invert them, then identify the inversion. You'll find that they always follow certain rules:

  • the number of an interval and its inversion will always add up to 9. So a second inverts to a seventh, a third to a sixth and a fourth to a fifth.
  • A major interval inverts to a minor one, a perfect to a perfect, an augmented to a diminished and vice versa.

Interval inversion is very useful for identifying larger intervals, like 6ths and 7ths. Most people find those harder than 2nds and 3rd, but by inverting them, we can turn them into 2nds or 3rds. Then we can identify the inverted interval, and by following the rules above we then know what the original interval was.

Now that we know about interval inversion, let's think back to something we learned in section 3. When we looked at all the intervals in the C major scale, we found that all the 2nds, 3rds, 6ths and 7ths were major or minor, and all the 4ths and 5ths were perfect apart from B-F and F-B.

F-B is an augmented 4th B-F is a diminished 5th

F-B and B-F are the inversion of one another! They are an augmented 4th and the diminished 5th! All the other intervals are major, minor or perfect. If we invert them we have another major, minor or perfect interval. But these to are the only exceptions, which invert onto each other.

In an exam, I would encourage you to invert every sixth and seventh, use this as a second method of identifying the interval, and as a way to double check you answers.
