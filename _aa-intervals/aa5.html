Let's look at the C major scale again. This time, we're going to identify the intervals from note 1 of the scale (C) up to each of the other notes.

We can see that all the intervals are either perfect or major! If you are unsure about this go back and review the section where we identified all intervals in the C major scale.

This is very useful, because it is not only true of C major; it is true of any major scale. In any major scale, the interval from note 1 up to any of the other notes of the scale will always be perfect or major.

As an example, let's think about some notes in the E major scale.

  • E is note 1 of the scale.
  • B is note 5, and E - B is a perfect fifth.
  • C♯ is note 6, and E - C♯ is a major 6th.

If we know our major scales, this gives us a different method for identifying intervals. Let's use this to identify the interval G - E♭.

We wany to identify the interval G-E♭

The note E♭ is not in the G major scale, but the note E is. It is the sixth note of the scale, so D - E is a major 6th, which means that G - E♭ is a minor 6th.

G - E is a major 6th so G - E♭ is a minor 6th

Let's try a harder one.

let's try to identify the interval D♯-C

We could, in theory, use the D♯ major scale to identify this interval, but that would get very complicated. D♯ major is a scale you've probably never seen before. But we can instead use D major, which is a scale we are all more familiar with.

D major has 2 sharps in the key signature, so it has a C♯ as the 7th note. So we know that D-C♯ is a major 7th. We will work from that starting point and then add a natural to the C and a sharp to the D to get to the interval we wanted to identify.

D-C♯ is a major 7th D-C♮ is a minor 7th D♯-C♮ is a diminished 7th

If you are not very confident with your major scales, make sure you get better at them and come back to this method later.
